2 – Problem Analysis

An ability to use appropriate knowledge and skills to identify, formulate, analyze and solve complex engineering problems in order to reach substantiated conclusions. (Applies to “one correct answer” problems and open-ended design problems)

Indicator Unacceptable
1		 Below Expectations
2		 Meets Expectations
3		 Exceeds Expectations
4
PA 1 – Demonstrate ability to define an engineering problem. Problem is not correctly stated or identified. Dissection of the problem into key components contains inaccuracies or omissions: known or unknown information incorrectly identified, uncertain quantities not recognized, definition includes inappropriate or unnecessary assumptions OR lacks necessary assumptions. The key components of the problem are identified, including identification of known and unknown information, uncertain quantities. Assumptions are appropriate and justified. Meets expectations plus: Statement of problem demonstrates creativity or insight or synthesis of information from multiple sources.
PA 2 – Demonstrate ability to formulate a strategy to solve an engineering problem. No appropriate engineering principles or solution strategies are used. Engineering principles are incorrectly applied in one or more steps of the problem solution. OR Inappropriate or unnecessary assumptions are made. OR Solution plan is inefficient or not systematic. Appropriate engineering principles and solution strategies are applied to solve each of the component parts. AND Assumptions are appropriate and justified. AND Solution plan is efficient, systematic. Meets expectations, plus: Limitations of the models and strategies are identified, leading to possible alternatives.
PA 3 – Demonstrate ability to reach substantiated conclusions. No solution or incorrect solution. Correct method has been incorrectly applied leading to incorrect solution. No assessment of reasonableness of conclusion. Solution is complete AND essentially correct. Valid conclusions are drawn. Meets expectations, plus: Recognizes the limitations of the solution.


3 – Investigation

An ability to conduct investigations of complex problems by methods that include appropriate experiments, analysis and interpretation of data, and synthesis of information in order to reach valid conclusions.

Indicator Unacceptable
1		 Below Expectations
2		 Meets Expectations
3		 Exceeds Expectations
4
I 1 – Demonstrate ability to define and plan the investigation successfully (whether experimental or analytical). No meaningful implementation plan. Implementation plan contains significant flaws concerning proposed methods, tools, design OR assumptions. Implementation plan proposes appropriate methods, tools, design, AND assumptions. Meets expectations, plus: Limitations and implications are completely identified.
I 2 – Demonstrate ability to conduct an investigation successfully. Standard protocols ignored, leading to safety concerns. Standard protocols not appropriately implemented, raising safety concerns. Investigation conducted safely using standard protocols where available. Meets expectations, plus: Successfully develops new protocols to ensure possible safety concerns are addressed.
I 3 – Demonstrate ability to analyse and interpret data to reach valid conclusions. No meaningful analysis of data, leading to invalid conclusions. Conclusions are not justified by the data analysis presented OR experimental error and its impact not quantified. Conclusions justified by appropriate data analysis AND quantification of experimental error and its impact. Meets expectations, plus: Conclusions are framed in the context of prior knowledge and recommendations for future work are provided.


4 – Design

An ability to design solutions for complex, open-ended engineering problems and to design systems, components or processes that meet specified needs with appropriate attention to health and safety risks, applicable standards, and economic, environmental, cultural and societal considerations.

Indicator Unacceptable
1		 Below Expectations
2		 Meets Expectations
3		 Exceeds Expectations
4
D 1 – Demonstrate ability to frame a complex, open-ended design problem in engineering terms. Problem statement, objectives and constraints are missing or entirely incorrect. Problem statement is partially incorrect or incomplete OR Objectives and constraints are partially or incorrectly expressed in engineering terms. Problem statement is sufficient AND Objectives and constraints are correctly defined in engineering terms. Meets expectations, plus: Significance and nature of problem identified in context of societal and cultural considerations.
D 2 – Demonstrate ability to generate a diverse set of candidate engineering design solutions. No alternative solutions are developed. One or more alternatives are ignored OR developed concepts are based on a limited set of physical principles. Multiple alternative solutions are generated AND solutions are based on the principles embodied by the design objectives and constraints. Multiple concepts comprise a complete range of physical embodiments, some of them encompassing innovative “out of the box” solutions.
D 3 – Demonstrate ability to select candidate engineering design solutions for further development. The solution is chosen without any justification. Some justification is provided, but without consideration for ONE OF the following: Technical soundness, environmental impact, economics, public/user health & safety. Merit of alternative solutions is appropriately evaluated, leading to selection of preferred concept considering the following: Technical soundness, environmental impact, economics, public/user health & safety. Meets expectations, plus: Merits of alternative solutions is evaluated using higher-order system performance metrics.
D 4 – Demonstrate ability to advance an engineering design to a defined end state – completion. The design is not developed to a defined end state because little or no engineering analysis is conducted. The design is developed toward a defined end state but is incomplete OR based on flawed engineering analysis and/or judgment. The design is developed to the defined end state based on sound engineering analysis and judgment. Design performance assessed in the context of problem definition to identify potential improvements. Meets expectations, plus: Advanced validation conducted using analysis, testing, simulation. Compliance with relevant codes & standards is demonstrated.


5 – Use of Engineering Tools

An ability to create, select, apply, adapt, and extend appropriate techniques, resources, and modern engineering tools to a range of engineering activities, from simple to complex, with an understanding of the associated limitations. The scope of “Engineering Tools” (ET) includes • Basic and advanced numerical modelling tools (spreadsheets, Matlab) • Computer-aided Engineering (CAx) Tools (design software, finite element analysis, 3-D printing) • Conventional engineering databases and resources (data/formula tables in handbooks/catalogues, materials specifications)

Indicator Unacceptable
1		 Below Expectations
2		 Meets Expectations
3		 Exceeds Expectations
4
ET 1 – Demonstrates ability to identify and select appropriate engineering tool(s) and resources Applicability of an ET to a problem not recognized: ET not used! Improper ET selected because underlying mathematical AND/OR engineering fundamentals not recognized. ET selection justified by comprehension of the underlying mathematical AND/OR engineering fundamentals. Meets expectation, plus: Limitations of the selected tool are identified and assessed.
ET 2 – Demonstrates ability to apply appropriate engineering tool(s) and resources ET solution is wildly wrong ET solution is wrong AND/OR not checked by independent calculation. ET solution is correct AND checked by independent calculation Meets expectation, plus: Proficient in use of advanced engineering tool.
ET 3 – Demonstrates ability to create/develop/adapt appropriate engineering tools Unable to create simple Engineering tools or modify existing tools to a new purpose. Creation/adaption of ET is incomplete AND/OR yields incorrect results. Creation/adaptation of ET is adequate AND yields correct results. Meets expectations, plus: ET created/adapted is sufficiently documented (or intuitive) that others can use it readily.

7 – Communication Skills

An ability to communicate complex engineering concepts within the profession and with society at large. Such ability includes reading, writing, speaking and listening, and the ability to comprehend and write effective reports and design documentation, and to give and effectively respond to clear instructions.

Indicator Unacceptable
1		 Below Expectations
2		 Meets Expectations
3		 Exceeds Expectations
4
CS 1 – Demonstrate ability to follow instructions (listening and reading for comprehension). Unable to discern main message of reading material Comprehends main message of reading material, but unable to provide specific relevant details of the material read Demonstrates comprehension of the primary message through appropriate action and/or response. Meets expectations, plus: Demonstrates effective synthesis of material read into current project or initiative.
CS 2 – Demonstrate the ability to communicate orally using appropriate materials, language, non-verbal communication and effective graphical tools. Poor quality presentation with ineffective materials; graphical tools not used; poor quality of spoken delivery Presentation not of a professional standard with marginal material; graphical tools only partially effective; marginal spoken delivery. Presentation of a professional standard using appropriate material that is tailored to target audience; effective use of graphics; professional quality spoken delivery. Meets expectations, plus: Presentation is enlightening and engaging.
CS 3 – Demonstrate ability to articulate ideas in writing using appropriate technical language, and effective graphical tools. Technical language is unclear, with much jargon, presentation is incoherent, spelling grammar and syntax mostly incorrect, graphical tools not used where appropriate. Technical language is sometimes unclear or inappropriate to the audience, ideas and arguments are unpersuasive; spelling grammar and syntax interfere with understanding; graphical tools used but only partly effective. Technical language is clear, and appropriate to the audience, ideas are persuasive, spelling, grammar and syntax are mostly correct, graphical tools are used effectively. Meets expectations plus: Written document is sufficiently engaging to be understood and interesting to readers from other disciplines

9 – Impact of Engineering on Society and the Environment

An ability to analyze social and environmental aspects of engineering activities. Such ability includes an understanding of the interactions that engineering has with the economic, social, health, safety, legal, and cultural aspects of society, the uncertainties in the prediction of such interactions; and the concepts of sustainable design and development and environmental stewardship.

Indicator Unacceptable
1		 Below Expectations
2		 Meets Expectations
3		 Exceeds Expectations
4
IESE 1 Ability to analyse the interactions of engineering with economic, social, health, safety, legal and cultural aspects of society. No awareness of impact of engineering on economic aspects of society. Unable to analyse effectively the impact of engineering on the society and environment. Able to quantify accurately the impact of engineering on the society and environment. Meets expectations plus: Analysis takes into account the uncertainty of prediction of interaction with society and environment.
IESE 2 – Demonstrate understanding of the concept of sustainable design and development. Sustainability ignored when making engineering decisions. Proposed engineering solutions address a small subset of the relevant sustainable design and development issues. Sustainability of proposed solutions is appropriately quantified and addressed. Meets expectations, plus: Aspects of proposed engineering solutions encompass innovative “green”/sustainable design principles, materials and/or technologies.
IESE 3 – Demonstrate understanding of the concept of environmental stewardship. Environmental stewardship ignored when making engineering decisions. Proposed engineering solutions address a small subset of the relevant environmental stewardship considerations. Environmental stewardship is appropriately quantified and addressed in engineering solutions. Meets expectations, plus: Aspects of proposed engineering solutions encompass innovative environmental stewardship initiatives.
